Chapter 01 · 0.00 AU

The Sun

Everything begins in a furnace. The Sun holds 99.86 percent of the solar system's mass, fusing six hundred million tonnes of hydrogen every second. Its light crosses the gulf to Earth in 8 minutes, 20 seconds — and its wind, a breath of charged particles at a million miles per hour, inflates a bubble around everything you have ever known.

Inner Worlds

Four rocky survivors circle close to the fire. Mercury, a scorched iron heart. Venus, smothered under clouds at 464 °C. Earth, improbably wet, improbably alive. Mars, rust-red and patient. Between them: hard vacuum, raw sunlight, and the thin bright arcs of their orbits.

The Belt

A ring of rubble that never became a world. More than a million asteroids larger than a kilometre drift here, yet their combined mass is barely 3 percent of the Moon's. Ceres, the largest, hides a salty brine beneath its crust. Spacecraft cross the belt without ever seeing a single stone.

Chapter 04 · 5.2 – 30 AU

Gas Giants

Beyond the frost line, worlds swell to impossible sizes. Jupiter could swallow 1,300 Earths; its great storm has raged for centuries. Saturn's rings span 282,000 kilometres yet average just ten metres thick. Uranus and Neptune roll on through the ice-blue dark, thirty times farther from the Sun than home.

Chapter 05 · ~121 AU

The Heliopause

Here the solar wind finally stalls against the breath of other stars. On 25 August 2012 — thirty-five years after launch — Voyager 1 crossed this boundary at 121 AU, the first human-made object to leave the Sun's bubble. Its signal, travelling at light speed, now takes almost a full day to reach us. The Sun ends. The galaxy begins.

You are 121 AU from home. Everything you have ever loved lies sunward of this line.

The heliopause is not a wall but a pressure balance — the point where the Sun's wind can push no further. Voyager 1 still calls back from beyond it, on 470 watts and falling.
