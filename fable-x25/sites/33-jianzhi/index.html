窗花 · Window Flowers

Pasted to the lattice, lit from behind

At the turning of the lunar year the cuts are pressed to paper windows, where the low winter sun pours through every hole and sets the red aglow.

Each pane is a fresh generation — seeded, symmetric, unrepeatable. The negative space is the art: what the scissors remove is what the light comes through.

傳統 · The Tradition

Fifteen centuries of subtraction

Jiǎnzhǐ is the oldest of China's folk arts to survive on paper — a craft of removal, where beauty is what remains after the cut.

剪紙 · The Cut

Paper & Blade

Since the invention of paper in the Han, village artisans — most of them women — have folded thin sheets and worked them with scissors and fine knives. Fold once and cut, and a single motion is answered on the far side of the crease; fold again, and again, and the pattern multiplies into a rosette.

There is no undo. The craft lives entirely in what is taken away.

窗花 · Window Flowers

Light Through Paper

The most beloved cuts are 窗花 — window flowers — pasted onto the paper lattices of northern courtyards for the Spring Festival. Backlit by winter light, the negative space glows, and the whole room is scattered with red.

They are meant to be temporary: hung for the new year, weathered, and cut anew when spring returns.

紅與對稱 · Red & Symmetry

Fortune, Balance, the Turning Year

Red is the colour of fortune and warding, of weddings and the new year. Symmetry is balance made visible — the folded cut guarantees it, the blade unable to favour one side over the other.

福 for fortune, 囍 for doubled joy, the peony for wealth, cranes and clouds for long life: every motif is a wish, multiplied by the fold.

Fold once, cut once — and luck blooms 六 sixfold, 八 eightfold, 十二 twelvefold.
