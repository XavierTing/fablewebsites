传统·ON READING THE NIGHT

为何是周公why the Duke of Zhou, and how to read a dream well

一 · I

周公其人Who the Duke was

周公旦 — the Duke of Zhou — was regent and statesman at the founding of the Zhou dynasty, three thousand years ago, and for centuries the very emblem of wise, ordered rule. So beloved was the memory of his good governance that Confucius, feeling his own powers fade, sighed that he no longer dreamt of the Duke of Zhou.

The dream-book was never truly written by his hand. His name was lent to it the way a temple lends its roof — a mark of authority under which the folk wisdom of a hundred generations could gather and keep warm.

二 · II

梦为兆The dream as omen

In the old cosmology the sleeping self loosens from the body and wanders where the waking self cannot. What it meets there — a dragon, a flood, a lost tooth — was read as a message crossing between worlds: the ancestors speaking, the coming season announcing itself, the unseen order tipping its hand.

So the almanac learned its lovely counter-intuitions. A coffin is promotion; blood is wealth; muck is money; weeping is joy on the way. The night, it insists, rarely means the thing it shows you.

三 · III

镜，非谶A mirror, not a prophecy

Read gently, a dream almanac is not a fortune-teller but a mirror held at an angle. The symbol it names — the falling, the being-chased, the tooth coming loose in your hand — is a question your daylight self has been too busy to ask.

Take the omen lightly and the reflection seriously. No line here can tell you what will happen. Every line can tell you what you already feel, in a language old enough to be trusted. Read it, then set it down, and go and live the day.

四 · IV

蝴蝶之梦Zhuangzi's butterfly

Long ago, the philosopher Zhuangzi dreamed he was a butterfly, drifting and content, knowing nothing of Zhuangzi. Then he woke and was, unmistakably, himself again.

不知周之梦为蝴蝶与，蝴蝶之梦为周与。
Now — did Zhuang Zhou dream he was the butterfly? Or does the butterfly dream it is Zhuang Zhou?

He never decided, and neither should you need to. Hold your dreams that lightly. The wonder was never in knowing which world is the real one — only in having, for one night, been carried gently through both.
