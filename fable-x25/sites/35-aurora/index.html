What you are actually seeing

A spark thrown off the meeting of a star and a shield.

The colour overhead is not paint and not fire. It is a collision — ninety-three million miles in the making — that you happened to be standing under.

The sun is always exhaling. Roughly a million tonnes of charged particles leave it every second and ride outward on the solar wind, crossing the dark between us at some four hundred kilometres a second.

Earth meets them with a magnetic field — an invisible shield that bends the stream around the planet and funnels the strays down toward the poles, where the shield dips inward and finally touches the top of the air.

A hundred kilometres up, those particles strike oxygen and nitrogen. Each atom takes the blow, holds it for a fraction of a second, then hands the energy back as a single note of light.

Oxygen answers in green, and — higher, thinner, rarer — in deep red. Nitrogen fringes the falling curtains violet. Every ribbon is the visible edge of atoms relaxing, all at once, in the cold.
